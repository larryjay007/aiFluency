Case Study 01

The "0 rows removed" result that looked fine — until it wasn't.

From an early exercise on a 30,000-page anonymized content dataset: a correlation stayed identical before and after a filter. The stable number looked like an answer. It was actually a question I hadn't asked yet.

Check 1 — Why didn't the filter change anything?

What I saw

An initial pass measured the correlation between a keyword's search volume and the actual page impressions it produced. The result was near zero (0.001) — search volume barely predicted real traffic.

What I questioned

The next step was to re-run that same correlation, but restricted to only pages that had recorded at least one real impression — to see if removing "invisible" pages changed the relationship.

What I found

The filter removed exactly zero rows. 30,000 pages before, 30,000 pages after. The correlation stayed at 0.001, unchanged.

Why it mattered

An unchanged result is easy to read as "the relationship holds either way." That reading would have been wrong. The real explanation was structural: this dataset was already pre-filtered to only include pages with measured impressions — there was no zero-impression subset left to remove in the first place.

The outcome

The write-up was corrected before it went anywhere: not "filtering confirms no relationship," but "the filter was a no-op on this dataset, so this comparison can't confirm anything either way." Less satisfying. More true.

Check 2 — Does a model actually use the feature I hand it?

What I saw

A shallow decision tree, trained to flag declining pages, split first on a page's recorded impressions — a reasonable, expected first split.

What I questioned

I swapped that feature out for a different one — a page's engagement rate — assuming it was at least as informative, maybe more.

What I found

Engagement rate never appeared in either split of the resulting tree. The model preferred two other signals — a page's average search position and its content age — over the feature I'd expected it to lean on.

Why it mattered

It would have been easy to assume a plausible-sounding feature was doing work just because it was available. The tree's actual structure said otherwise — and the only way to know that was to read it, not just trust that adding a "good" feature helped.

This is the kind of check I run on every result before I trust it — not "does this look right," but "what would have to be true for this to look right, and is it?"
